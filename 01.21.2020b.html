The Why

So if you've checked out my About Me section, then you'll probably know most of what I'm about to tell you, but for those who don't, I'll explain it here.

The reason I started this blog goes back a little ways, like back to the beginning of my coding days, about 13 years ago. See, I fell in love with coding my freshman year of high school. I'm from a small town in Alaska called Homer, and while I love many things about my home state, there wasn't a push for technically inclined people when I lived there, especially in my hometown. So I ended up diverted from the path I wanted to take to appease my family and their friends. I wouldn't necessarily say I regret that decision because it led to some great experiences that I wouldn't trade for the world, but I wondered for a long time what would happen were I to pick up my old love again.

After breaking out into the world and starting my own family, I learned of some limitations my body was putting on me. After working two to three jobs and raising children, I had accelerated my genetic condition, which I had no clue about at this time. This started a long process of me trying to find answers and the medical community just shrugging and telling me nothing was broken, so they couldn't fix it. I didn't want a fix, I wanted to understand, so I continually searched until one of my doctors finally figured out what was happening and helped me get my diagnosis. In 2014, I found out that I have Ehlers-Danlos syndrome. Ehlers-Danlos syndrome is a connective tissue disorder that is caused by the body's DNA not having the correct coding to make collagen, therefore it makes faulty collagen and affects every part of your body using this protein, from your tendons and ligaments to your digestive and neurological systems. It's a big syndrome with a lot of comorbidities. In other words, it causes a lot of other issues.

I was devastated. I saw no way out. My body was going through what we in the chronic illness community call a flare, and I felt like my life was over. I mourned my dreams for myself, my goals, my life as I knew it. I mourned for my children, as Ehlers-Danlos syndrome has a 50/50 chance of being inherited. I was unlucky and have a 100% success rate for passing it on. Once we found out my children were affected, I set my mind to finding SOMETHING that could help, or at least keep them from getting as bad as I had. I met with many different doctors from many different specialties, and eventually, we started being able to manage my symptoms as well as get ahead of the children being symptomatic, and gradually I saw a light at the end of the dark tunnel.

My husband started really encouraging me to find things that I was still able to do even on my worst days, and I tried many with no fruition, until it finally occurred to me that I still loved coding. About the same time, I saw an advertisement on Facebook for a nifty little app called Career Karma. The advertisement itself was gorgeous, and I became curious. SoI downloaded it. This was the defining moment that changed my life for the better.

Career Karma is an app that connects you with people and resources to achieve your coding goals. Through their #21DayCKChallenge and their squads, you're connected with thousands of people who range from just starting coding to senior software developers and more. They also help connect you with boot camps that help you learn coding and the pertinent information you need to pursue your career. The idea is that you use the resources and better your life, and in turn, you help others on the app do the same -- hence Career Karma.

Through this app, I was challenged but also encouraged. Since beginning my 21-day challenge I have not stopped pursuing my education in coding and am happy to announce that as of January 28th, 2020, I will be starting a yearlong boot camp at Kenzie Academy studying to be a UX (user experience) engineer. This is the start of a lifelong dream I had set aside but never forgotten, and for the first time in a long time, I feel free. This is the beginning of my career, and I couldn't be more excited. It's also something for my children to see and know that this disability may change a lot of things, but it doesn't change who we are at the core, and it definitely doesn't define our worth to the world.

So there it is. My why is complex on the best of days, but I think that makes it more interesting in the long run. I'm here not only to help myself and my children, but to hopefully show others that no matter how daunting the task may seem, that with a little help we can all get to our dreams. It's not easy, but no one promised us easy when they told us to chase our dreams, just that it would be rewarding. So for now, I'm going to keep documenting this journey, sharing some tips and tricks I have found and have yet to find along the way, so that the next person might find all of this just a little bit more accessible. After all, isn't helping each other what being human is all about?
