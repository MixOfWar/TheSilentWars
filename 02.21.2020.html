Brain What?

Brainstorming, brings up a lot of mixed feelings and experiences doesn't it? Most people remember brainstorming in grade school but the fact of the matter is it's become a lot more refined than it used to be. Today I'm going to go over my top three favorite styles of brainstorming and why they're my favorites. I usually use all three when brainstorming for a project.


Method 1: Brain Dump

Brain Dumps are one of the few things that are very similar to what it sounds like. The idea behind brain dumps is to get out every thought related to the project or product, both good and bad. Take a piece of paper (or a few) and write out every idea that comes to mind, if you stall reread what you have and continue on. In this way you can really let the creativity flow without limiting yourself or others to the constraints of what you think the solution should look like. Brain dumps also spark conversations about what you're looking for in a "good" solution, and what you aren't looking for i.e. a "bad" solution. It is especially key to remember that sometimes the "bad" idea is what sparks the final solution, sometimes the "bad" idea sets new parameters that you hadn't previously considered and by letting that out others can build it into something amazing.


Method 2: Worst Possible Idea

The idea behind the Worst Possible Idea is to define the parameters of what you do not want included in the solution. For this brainstorming method you really just write or talk out the worst possible solutions and use them to refine the ideas in method 1. By doing this you're looking at the good and the bad of the solutions proposed and figuring out ways to get around the bad, but expand upon the good. It's a beautiful refinement technique and can help lower the tension allowing for more creativity to flow.


Method 3: Mind Map

For this method we are going to take the ideas from method 1 that we didn't lose in method 2 and define them by their features. Combine the solutions that are similar or refer to the same feature, then write them all out in a way that flows. When you make a mind map you start with the problem in the middle, then put the needs/features as offshoots of the problem, then put the solutions under the needs/features they deal with. This helps to refine what each solution pertains to, you can condense the solutions, and really see the beginnings of the ultimate solution. This will also be the point where you can really iterate the final idea forms of the solutions and start getting a bigger picture of the entire solution in and of itself.


By combining these methods, whether you're working with a team, or individually, you'll find that you can really refine your ideas and come up with a workable solution. Using these methods within the design process can help you move onto the next step without getting stuck on the ideation phase. Hopefully you all find this useful, as for me I'll be enjoying my weekend and getting ready for the next phase of this learning process. Enjoy your weekend!
