Let's start a dialogue ...

The art of conversation isn't something that comes easily to everyone. In fact most people struggle in some way with conversing, especially with strangers. However, one of the biggest parts of doing research in design is interviewing. Interviewing allows us a peer inside the lives of the people we are trying to help, it allows us to look at the world through their eyes and get a better grasp of how we can help without our own views getting in the way. In this way it is a very important part of the design process and one that cannot be easily skipped over. Without understanding the people we are trying to help, how can we even begin to understand the problem. The simple answer? We can't.

Now there are a multitude of different ways that you can approach an interview, you can aim for a casual conversation, or you can aim for a more aggressive question after question based interview, it's going to depend on your style. Today, I'm going to talk about my experience with interviews and what worked best for me this week. I feel like at this point I shouldn't have to clarify this but I'm going to anyway. I AM NOT AN EXPERT IN UX DESIGN, I AM A STUDENT PASSING ON MY EXPERIENCES. I just feel like it's important to remember that just because something works for me, or I like a specific thing doesn't mean that it will work for everyone, or that it's what you should do, and definitely doesn't make it the industry standard..

My favorite approach so far, and I've tried a few, is just the casual conversation approach. This can probably be attributed to the fact that I am a pretty outgoing person but I have found that even when I'm interviewing people who are introverted that this has produced the most in depth answers and really opens up the ability to truly empathize with the people that I am interviewing. In this way I can really focus on their answers using my questions as more of a guideline, while really getting to the core of their perspective. When you're trying to solve someone else's problem you have to be able to put yourself in their shoes and walk around a bit. You need to be able to look at the issue from a place outside your own views, and set aside what you think you know about the solution. In this way you can really get to the core of the issue(s) and start to address what the people need, and how you can make that happen, which is, after all, the point of interviewing.

Now there are a few things you have to keep in mind. When you conduct an interview people, no matter what you do, will try to produce their best self, so what they say and what they do can differ at times, and that's okay, as long as you're aware of it. It's also important to remember that generally speaking the people you're interviewing aren't designers, so don't take their feedback as gospel. I have personally found it interesting to figure out what people have used prior, how that did or didn't work for them, and what changes they think they would have made, but if you aren't careful that can lead you into a trap of features that don't really work in the long run. However, letting people talk about their ideas can also help you backtrack to the problem in a different way, letting you glean more information than just asking the standard interview questions.

Other than that, it's really about making sure that you can record the interview to really see people's responses, making sure that your questions are giving you a good picture of the problem from the people's view, and just being thorough. All of these things together will complete your picture and give you the best opportunity to be the problem solver you're trying to be. After all, UX Design/Engineering is really just the ability to solve problems in the most efficient way possible. In this way you're not only good at your job but you're doing a valuable service both for the company you work for, and the people that company serves.

Now I'm off to write more notes, hopefully conduct more interviews, and start working on solving the problem I'm currently working on. See you all next week!
