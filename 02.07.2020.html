I'm Sensing A Theme ...

Another week down and I'm starting to recognize a theme at this school. I'm not only thoroughly enjoying my time within my class settings, but I'm actively learning each week and am almost constantly having fun. Now I like learning probably more than your average person. I consume information like most people breathe, however, it's been a while since I could honestly say that I was completely enjoying the method by which I am learning. There is so much that is challenging and difficult but it's all in a good way that pushes you outside your comfort zone and forces you to grow as you proceed through the class. Beyond that I've gotten to really know quite a few of my classmates and I really am enjoying all the different perspectives coming out of this class.

This week we did a design sprint in groups. The idea behind a design sprint is that sometimes we tend, as designers, to overthink things and spend way too much time working on a particular issue. In comes design sprints where you bust out an entire solution in a week by following a fluid game plan from start to finish. There's 5 stages to a design sprint and I'll do my best to explain the process.


Empathize:

I lovingly call this stage the research stage. See the idea is to set aside everything that you think you know about the problem and look at it with new eyes. Depending on the amount of time you have this stage can be very superficial, to a crazy amount of information. The important part is that you spend time gathering resources from different locations, it's okay if some of the information is the same but you really want to make sure you get a broad range of information to better help you to work on the next step.


Define:

This part of the process is about analyzing the information, really getting to the pit of the problem and to start assessing functions or features that will help your users combat the problem efficiently. This part does build some assumptions because you have to have something to test before you put it in front of people but that's okay because we are going to test those assumptions with actual people to see if we get it right. The more complete your information is from step 1, the better this information will be.


Ideate:

I think this is my favorite part, you let the creative juices flow and just try to solve the problem. Big ideas, small ideas, little features, big functions, just get those ideas out there. Then you take time to go over them, pick the ones that appear the most, the repeats and really look at the outliers and see which ones should just be included, which have cool elements that you might want to include, and which are just going to have to wait until another time. This process is so interesting because you have a bunch of people who just went over the same information and sometimes you come to similar conclusions and sometimes you end up on polar opposite ends, but it's all valid, and wonderful. Ok, nevermind, we all know this is my favorite part.


Prototype:

Now we get to build it, *insert evil laugh here*. No but really, you get to build a framing of how you and your team want the app to look. Here we're focusing on user interaction and showing them what you've come up with as a team. We want to focus on things they can give feedback on and the main aspects of the solution. You don't want to be overly detailed but you want to give enough information that people can really get a feel for how the solution is going to help them solve this particular problem and the key features/functions to make sure that it's accessible and user friendly. Remember that the main thing this will lead to is feedback and you'll be on the right track.


Test:

No this isn't the part where you take a test on it, I promise. This is the part where you take your prototype to people and see if they can navigate what you have created. Look for critics here, separate yourself from the product and really let people play with it, and figure it out. This is both the hardest part and also the most rewarding. See inevitably you haven't created a perfect product because that doesn't exist, but you will have created something tangible that people can really grasp and understand. TAKE NOTES!!!!! I cannot stress how important this is, and if at all possible record the interaction because watching people navigate your product is almost more important than what they say about it (almost). Then compile all this information and start making the changes needed, and really refining your product.


And that's it, from here you'll continue to refine the product and then get all your developers behind it and start producing the product. That's a fun sentence. All in all this process can put you through the ringer but it's also a WONDERFUL experience. If you go into it with an open mind and remember that the critics are not about you personally you will create an amazing product that can help many people, and they'll want to use it.

With that all said I'm going to go enjoy my weekend. I'm really looking forward to next week's curriculum and getting back to class. Hope you all get a chance to relax and really enjoy some time this weekend. Until next time.
